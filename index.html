🧠 Nervous System Structure

The Human Nervous System
Central Nervous System — Brain + Spinal Cord
Peripheral Nervous System
Somatic NS
Voluntary skeletal movement
Autonomic NS
Involuntary organs & glands
Sympathetic NS
Fight-or-flight
Parasympathetic NS
Rest-and-digest
  • CNS: Brain and spinal cord — processes, integrates, and coordinates all information.
  • PNS: All nerves outside the CNS connecting to body tissues, organs, muscles.
  • Somatic NS: Controls voluntary skeletal muscle movements (conscious control).
  • Autonomic NS: Controls involuntary functions (heart, digestion, glands) — operates largely unconsciously to maintain homeostasis.
  • Sympathetic NS: Activates fight-or-flight: ↑HR, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion, releases adrenaline.
  • Parasympathetic NS: Restores rest-and-digest: ↓HR, constricts pupils, stimulates digestion, conserves energy.
💡 Memory trick: Sympathetic = Stress. Parasympathetic = Peace.

⚡ Neurons & Neural Transmission

Three types of neurons:

  • Sensory neurons (afferent): Carry impulses FROM sensory receptors TO the CNS. Pseudo-unipolar — no dendrites, have receptive endings.
  • Motor neurons (efferent): Carry impulses FROM the CNS TO muscles/glands. Multipolar — terminal ends form neuromuscular junctions.
  • Interneurons: Connect sensory and motor neurons within the CNS. Found in the spinal cord.

Structure of a Neuron

  • Dendrites — receive incoming signals
  • Cell body (soma) — processes signals
  • Axon — transmits signal away from body
  • Myelin sheath — insulates axon; speeds conduction
  • Axon terminals — release neurotransmitters into synapse

Key Neurotransmitters

  • Glutamate — main excitatory NT; memory & learning
  • GABA — main inhibitory NT; relaxation, reduces anxiety
  • Dopamine — reward, motivation, movement
  • Serotonin — mood, sleep, wellbeing
  • Adrenaline — stress; activates SNS fight-or-flight
  • Noradrenaline — alertness, attention, stress arousal

Synaptic transmission: Action potential travels along axon → triggers release of neurotransmitters from vesicles into the synapse → NTs bind to receptors on postsynaptic membrane → signal is either excitatory (increases likelihood of firing) or inhibitory (decreases likelihood).

🔬 Synaptic plasticity: The ability of synaptic connections to change in strength over time due to experience (activity-dependent). Underlies learning and memory formation.

😰 Stress as a Psychological Concept

Stress is a state of psychological and physiological arousal arising from perceived demands that exceed available coping resources.

Types of Stress

  • Eustress — positive; motivating; short-term (e.g., sporting event)
  • Distress — negative; overwhelming; harmful long-term
  • Acute — intense, brief (e.g., near-miss accident)
  • Chronic — prolonged, ongoing (e.g., financial pressure)

Biological Response to Stress

  • SNS activation → adrenaline released (fight-or-flight)
  • HPA axis → cortisol released (sustained stress)
  • Cortisol suppresses immune system long-term
  • Chronic stress → physical illness, burnout

Lazarus & Folkman — Transactional Model (1984):

  • Primary appraisal: "Is this situation threatening?" → Irrelevant / Benign-positive / Stressful (harm/loss, threat, challenge)
  • Secondary appraisal: "Can I cope?" → Evaluating available coping resources (social support, skills, time)
  • Stress occurs when demands are appraised as exceeding available resources
  • Explains individual differences in stress — same event, different appraisals
📌 Selye's GAS (General Adaptation Syndrome):
Stage 1 — Alarm: Fight-or-flight activated; initial resistance dips then surges.
Stage 2 — Resistance: Body appears to cope; resources being depleted; cortisol elevated.
Stage 3 — Exhaustion: Resources depleted; illness, immune suppression, burnout.
GAS is biological; Lazarus & Folkman is cognitive/psychological.

Coping strategies:

  • Approach coping: Directly addressing the stressor — adaptive long-term
  • Avoidance coping: Avoiding or escaping the stressor — may reduce anxiety short-term but maladaptive long-term
  • Problem-focused: Changing the situation itself
  • Emotion-focused: Managing emotional response to the situation

📚 Learning

Learning is a relatively permanent change in behaviour (or knowledge/skills) that results from experience.

Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

  • UCS — unconditioned stimulus (naturally causes response)
  • UCR — unconditioned response (natural reflex)
  • NS → paired with UCS → becomes CS
  • CR — conditioned response to CS alone
  • Extinction: CS presented without UCS → CR weakens
  • Spontaneous recovery: CR reappears after rest
  • Generalisation: Similar stimuli elicit CR
  • Discrimination: Distinguish between similar stimuli

Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

  • Positive reinforcement: Add pleasant stimulus → ↑ behaviour
  • Negative reinforcement: Remove unpleasant stimulus → ↑ behaviour
  • Positive punishment: Add unpleasant stimulus → ↓ behaviour
  • Negative punishment: Remove pleasant stimulus → ↓ behaviour
  • Schedules: Fixed ratio, variable ratio, fixed interval, variable interval

Observational Learning (Bandura)

  • Attention: Observer must notice the model's behaviour
  • Retention: Observer must remember what was observed
  • Reproduction: Observer must be physically able to perform the behaviour
  • Motivation: Observer must want to perform the behaviour (vicarious reinforcement)
  • Behaviour more likely to be imitated if model is rewarded (vicarious reinforcement)
🧠 Neural basis of learning: Long-term potentiation (LTP) — repeated stimulation strengthens synaptic connections via glutamate and AMPA receptors. Basis of synaptic plasticity in learning.

🗂️ Memory

Memory is the process by which information is encoded, stored, and retrieved.

Atkinson-Shiffrin Model (Multi-Store)

  • Sensory memory: Brief, large capacity (iconic ~0.3s; echoic ~3–4s)
  • Short-term memory (STM): ~7±2 items; ~20s without rehearsal
  • Long-term memory (LTM): Potentially unlimited; permanent
  • Info moves STM→LTM via elaborative rehearsal

Types of Long-Term Memory

  • Explicit (declarative):
    • Episodic — personal events/experiences
    • Semantic — general facts/knowledge
  • Implicit (non-declarative):
    • Procedural — motor skills (e.g., riding a bike)
    • Classically conditioned responses

Processes of memory:

  • Encoding: Converting information into a form that can be stored (semantic, acoustic, visual)
  • Storage: Retaining encoded information over time
  • Retrieval: Accessing stored memories (recall, recognition, relearning)

Forgetting:

  • Interference: Proactive (old disrupts new) or Retroactive (new disrupts old)
  • Decay: Memory trace fades over time without use
  • Retrieval failure: Cue-dependent forgetting (tip-of-tongue)
  • Motivated forgetting: Repression of distressing memories
🔬 Neural basis of memory: Hippocampus — critical for consolidating explicit memories from STM→LTM. Amygdala — enhances memory consolidation for emotional events (explains flashbulb memories).

😴 Consciousness & Sleep

Consciousness is awareness of internal and external stimuli — a spectrum from full alertness to deep sleep/unconscious states.

  • Normal waking consciousness (NWC): Alert, organised, purposeful. EEG shows beta waves (high frequency, low amplitude).
  • Altered states of consciousness (ASC): Any state that differs from NWC — sleep, anaesthesia, meditation, hypnosis, substances.

Sleep Stages

  • NREM Stage 1: Light sleep; alpha → theta waves; hypnic jerks; easily woken
  • NREM Stage 2: Sleep spindles & K-complexes; theta waves; harder to wake
  • NREM Stage 3: Deep/slow-wave sleep; delta waves; essential for physical restoration
  • REM sleep: Rapid eye movement; brain activity like NWC; vivid dreams; memory consolidation; muscle atonia

Measuring Sleep

  • EEG — electroencephalogram; measures brain wave activity
  • EOG — electrooculogram; measures eye movement
  • EMG — electromyogram; measures muscle activity
  • Sleep diary — self-report; records sleep times, quality
  • Actigraphy — wrist-worn device; measures movement patterns
  • Video monitoring — observational record

Sleep-wake cycle regulation:

  • Circadian rhythm: ~24-hour biological cycle regulating sleep/wake; controlled by the SCN (suprachiasmatic nucleus) in the hypothalamus
  • Melatonin: Released by pineal gland in darkness; promotes sleep onset
  • Ultradian rhythm: ~90-minute sleep cycle repeated throughout the night
  • Sleep pressure (adenosine): Builds up during waking; cleared during sleep
🌙 Sleep across the lifespan: Newborns need ~16hrs/day; adolescents need ~8–10hrs and experience circadian phase delay (natural tendency to sleep later); adults ~7–9hrs; older adults get less SWS and more fragmented sleep.

⚠️ Sleep Disorders & Deprivation

Sleep Deprivation Effects

  • Impaired cognitive function & decision-making
  • Reduced attention and concentration
  • Emotional dysregulation; irritability
  • Microsleeps (brief involuntary sleep episodes)
  • Weakened immune function
  • Cumulative sleep debt

Sleep Disorders

  • Insomnia — difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep
  • Sleep apnoea — repeated breathing cessation during sleep; disrupts SWS
  • Narcolepsy — sudden muscle weakness (cataplexy); excessive daytime sleepiness
  • REM sleep behaviour disorder — acting out dreams; loss of muscle atonia
  • Sleepwalking — NREM Stage 3 parasomnia
⚠️ Know the difference between total sleep deprivation (no sleep at all) and partial sleep deprivation (consistently less than needed). Effects are cumulative and dose-dependent.

💚 Mental Wellbeing

Mental wellbeing exists on a continuum — from thriving to serious mental illness. It is influenced by biological, psychological, and social factors (biopsychosocial model).

Dimensions of Wellbeing

  • Physical wellbeing — bodily health, sleep, nutrition, exercise
  • Psychological wellbeing — emotional regulation, self-efficacy, positive emotions, sense of meaning
  • Social wellbeing — relationships, belonging, community connection

Social & Emotional Wellbeing (SEWB)

  • Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander holistic concept of wellbeing
  • Encompasses connection to: land, culture, spirituality, ancestry, family, community
  • Wellbeing cannot be understood in isolation from Country and culture
  • Colonisation, intergenerational trauma as key risk factors

Internal and external protective factors:

  • Internal: Resilience, coping skills, optimism, self-efficacy, emotional intelligence
  • External: Social support networks, access to healthcare, safe environment, positive relationships
💡 Mental health continuum: Mental health is not simply the absence of disorder — it ranges from languishing → moderate → flourishing.

🕷️ Specific Phobia — Biopsychosocial Model

Specific phobia is an irrational, persistent, and excessive fear of a specific object/situation that causes significant distress and avoidance.

Biological Factors

  • GABA dysfunction: Reduced GABA activity → increased neural excitability → anxiety
  • Long-term potentiation (LTP): Fear memories are strengthened via LTP in the amygdala
  • Genetic predisposition: Heritable tendency toward anxiety disorders

Psychological Factors

  • Classical conditioning: Neutral stimulus becomes feared via pairing with aversive UCS
  • Operant conditioning: Avoidance negatively reinforces phobia (removes anxiety)
  • Cognitive biases: Catastrophising, attention biases toward threat

Social Factors & Treatment

  • Social factors: Observational learning (modelling fear); environmental triggers; stigma around seeking help
  • Biological: Anti-anxiety medication (benzodiazepines, which enhance GABA activity)
  • Psychological: Systematic desensitisation (gradual exposure + relaxation); CBT (cognitive restructuring)
  • Social: Psychoeducation; reducing stigma; group therapy; community support

🔬 Research Methods Overview

Scientific Methodologies

  • Experiment: Manipulates IV, measures DV, controls extraneous variables
  • Case study: In-depth study of one individual/group
  • Observational study: Naturalistic or structured; no manipulation
  • Survey/Questionnaire: Self-report data; large samples
  • Correlational study: Measures relationship between variables; no causation

Variables

  • IV (Independent Variable): What is manipulated
  • DV (Dependent Variable): What is measured
  • Extraneous variable (EV): Any variable other than IV that could affect DV
  • Confounding variable: An EV that DID systematically affect the DV
  • Operationalisation: Defining variables in measurable terms

Sampling

  • Random sampling: Every member of population has equal chance of selection — most representative
  • Stratified sampling: Population divided into subgroups; sampled proportionally
  • Convenience sampling: Easily accessible participants — most common but least representative
  • Random allocation: Randomly assigning participants to conditions (reduces participant-related bias)

Data & Analysis

  • Qualitative data: Non-numerical; descriptive; rich detail (e.g., interviews)
  • Quantitative data: Numerical; measurable; allows statistical analysis
  • Mean, median, mode: Measures of central tendency
  • Range, standard deviation: Measures of spread/variability
  • Outlier: Data point that is far from the others

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

  • Informed consent: Participants must be fully informed and voluntarily agree to participate
  • Voluntary participation: Right to withdraw at any time without penalty
  • Confidentiality: Personal data kept private
  • Anonymity: Participants cannot be identified from results
  • Deception: Only used when necessary; must be followed by debriefing
  • Debriefing: Explaining the true nature of the study after completion
  • Beneficence: Research must benefit society; minimise harm
  • Non-maleficence: Do not harm participants

Reliability vs Validity

  • Reliability: Consistency of results — same results if repeated
  • Validity: Accuracy — measuring what it claims to measure
  • Internal validity: IV caused the change in DV (not other factors)
  • External validity: Results can be generalised to broader population

Experimental Design

  • Independent groups: Different participants in each condition — avoids order effects; needs random allocation
  • Repeated measures: Same participants in all conditions — reduces participant variability; risk of order effects
  • Matched participants: Pairs matched on relevant variables
  • Counterbalancing: Order of conditions varied to minimise order effects
⚠️ Common confounds: Placebo effect, experimenter effect, demand characteristics, order effects. Always consider these when evaluating research.

Exam Tips

Strategies to maximise your SAC and exam marks

🎯

Read the Command Term First

Before answering, underline the command term. This determines how much depth is required. "Define" = 1–2 sentences. "Explain" = mechanism + why/how. "Evaluate" = strengths AND limitations.

📖

Apply to the Scenario

In SACs and exams, marks are almost always for application, not just definition. Name the concept → define it briefly → apply it to the specific scenario given in the question stem.

🧬

Nervous System Questions

  • Always specify which branch (SNS vs PNS vs somatic vs autonomic)
  • Name the neurotransmitter AND state its effect on the neuron/behaviour
  • Use action words: "activates", "inhibits", "increases", "decreases"
  • Distinguish excitatory vs inhibitory effects
😰

Stress Questions

  • State which model you are applying (Lazarus & Folkman vs Selye's GAS)
  • For Lazarus: address BOTH primary AND secondary appraisal
  • For GAS: name the stage and describe its physiological features
  • Cortisol ≠ adrenaline — different pathways!
📚

Learning & Memory

  • For classical conditioning: identify UCS, UCR, CS, CR explicitly
  • For operant conditioning: state what was added/removed AND the effect on behaviour
  • For memory: link structure (hippocampus, amygdala) to function
  • Distinguish implicit vs explicit memory clearly
🔬

Research Methods

  • Always operationalise variables — IV and DV must be measurable
  • Distinguish extraneous vs confounding variable (one COULD affect, one DID)
  • State WHY a design feature (e.g. random allocation) reduces a specific type of bias
  • Link ethical guideline to specific concern in the scenario
✍️

Extended Response Writing

  • Open with a clear direct statement — not re-quoting the question
  • Structure: Define → Explain mechanism → Apply to scenario → Evaluate (if needed)
  • Use psychological terminology throughout
  • Each paragraph = one main idea, well linked
⚠️

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing somatic and autonomic NS
  • Saying cortisol and adrenaline are the same thing
  • Forgetting secondary appraisal in Lazarus & Folkman
  • Describing instead of explaining when "explain" is asked
  • Not naming the memory store (STM/LTM) when discussing memory
  • Forgetting muscle atonia in REM sleep
💡

Biopsychosocial Answers

Many questions (especially on phobia and mental wellbeing) require you to address all three dimensions. Structure your answer clearly: Bio → name specific biological factor (e.g. GABA dysfunction). Psycho → psychological mechanism. Social → social or environmental factor.
